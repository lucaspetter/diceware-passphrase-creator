Diceware Passphrase Instructions
How to use dice to create secure passphrases.

⚀ ⚁ ⚂ ⚃ ⚄ ⚅

Version 1.0, 3 June 2015.

This webpage explains how you can generate secure, random passphrases using 6-sided dice and the Diceware word list. The longer and more random your passphrase, the more secure it is, and the more difficult it will be for an attacker to guess it. By rolling dice to select words from a list, you can easily produce a very long passphrase that is still memorable. Furthermore, by using different passphrases for every account, rather than using the same one everywhere, you can prevent having all of your accounts compromised if an attacker learns the passphrase for one of them.

There is a good reason dice are used instead of having a computer generate random numbers. Computers are designed to be predictable, not random, which is exactly what you don't want when creating a secure passphrase. Dice are reliably unpredictable, making them perfect for creating passphrases.

This webpage is designed to be self-contained — it's a single file that doesn't load any additional resources, so you can use it offline and easily share it with other people.

1. Decide how many words you need

The more words in your passphrase, the more secure it is. Generally, 3 words or fewer is unsafe, 4-6 words is safe for most uses, and 7 or more is very secure.

3. Use the numbers to look up the words

Look up each five digit number from the previous step in the list further down on this page, then find the word next to it. When you are done, the words that you have found are your new passphrase.

4. Optionally add random numerals or special characters

If your passphrase is long enough, there is no need for adding special characters (eg. ~!@#$%^&*) or numbers (0123456789) into your passphrase; a long passphrase composed only of words made up from alphabetical letters may be strong enough on its own. In fact, adding numbers or special characters to your passphrase will make it less memorable, which may not be desirable. Furthermore, the Diceware list already includes many numbers and special characters, so adding more numbers or characters may be redundant.

For example: "fudgeclerksellgrantwane" versus "fudge@clerk%sell!9167"

However, some websites or service providers require you to include numbers or special characters in your passphrase. If you need special characters, use the button below to generate random ones. If you need to have numbers in your passphrase, you should ideally use a 10-sided die to randomly select from numbers 0-9. 5. Save the passphrase somewhere safe

There are many places you can keep passphrases: in a passphrase manager application, on a piece of paper that is stored somewhere safe (not out in the open on your desk!), or simply memorized in your mind.

Passphrase manager programs are recommended because they keep an encrypted, searchable database of all your passphrases; the only passphrase you actually need to remember is the the master one that unlocks the database.
